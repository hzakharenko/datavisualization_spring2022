An evolving music taste:

How the coronavirus pandemic changed the way people listen to music

by Hanna Zakharenko

Art is a reflection of what is going on in the world. Historically, music taste and the song content have been shaped by important events. In 1985, the Live Aid charity concert reached 1.9 billion people around the world to help raise money for Ethiopian famine relief. Alongside big events to support causes, songs have been written about major conflicts. John Lennon wrote “Imagine” as a wish for world peace. “Respect” by Aretha Franklin was a powerful anthem for both civil rights and feminist movements.

The relationship between music and conflict has long been explored in history, especially around global events and war. In “Music in War, Music for Peace: A Review Article,” from the ethnomusicology journal, John Morgan O’Connell performed a colloquium where he found that: “musicians employed music to communicate multiple interpretations of a peaceful solution, using musical performance as a medium for addressing the diverse aspirations of distinctive audiences. In this respect, a peaceful song for one person could be a warlike song for another.”

Although it has been only two years since the coronavirus outbreak was declared a pandemic, music taste has already begun to shift. Some of these occurrences are likely just a growing change that has been happening as music taste and the way we listen to music (i.e. streaming, playing records, buying CDs, etc.) develops over time. Others may be a direct reflection of what is happening in the world. Regardless, it is compelling to look at these data side by side and see how they might already play a role together. This visual story explores that relationship.

Looking forward, it will be interesting to see how music changes into the 2020s, and if the changes to the music industry we’ve seen so far throughout the pandemic are permanent, or merely a reflection of emotions during the pandemic.
